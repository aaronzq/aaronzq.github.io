Widefield vs. SPIM

17 Dec, 2017 · Read in about 3 min · (453 Words)
SPIM widefield

SPIM: selective plane illumination microscope
If we use gaussian beam as illumination light, then the resolution of z axis is limited by the thickness of the beam in z direction. Note that the effective illumination region of the light is in the rayleigh distance, which by the way is proportional to the beam’s waist width^2, namely the thickness^2 of the beam as we mention above. So this distance decides the SPIM’s field of view. And as it increase, SPIM’s z resolution get worse.
And we notice that, for a normal widefield microscope the depth of view is decided by the Objective as the following:
> depth = lambda*n/NA^2
Take an example, if we use an high NA objective 40X1.0, then we could have depth as little as 0.5um, far less than SPIM’s thickness, then what is the advantage of SPIM compared to simple widefield?

First, the depth of view can surely decide the widefield microscope’s resolution in z axis, but the resolution doesn’t bring image with high contrast and SNR, due to the influence of the other defouced light. If we take a look as SPIM’s raw image, we would think it is rather clean. On the contrary, the widefield’s raw image is quite blurred.
Actually, there’s so-called deconvolution microscope which does the sectioning job just using high NA objective only. And it then followed the 3D deconvolution algorithm to eliminate the defocused light.
So, we do admit that the widefield has smaller resolution compared to gaussian SPIM under high maginification and NA objective. But we have to post-pocessing the image before it can reconstruct a good 3D object.
Second, the NA of the objective is nearly proportional to the magnification. But the thickness of gaussian beam is proportional to (rayleigh distance)^(½). Note that both magnification of objective and beam’s rayleigh distance are directly linked to the field of view. This is to say that, as the FOV goes larger, the required rayleigh distance goes larger, the required magnification goes less(while the CMOS size is limited), and the thickness of beam in SPIM goes bigger, but slower than widefield’s DOF. This is when SPIM outwins widefield in z resolution. It can be well illustrated by the following figure.

Fieldin1x = 13e6; %The original width of the field in nm
lambda = 488; % The laser's wavelength
M = [1,2,4,10,15,20,25,30,35,40]; % Magnification of the Objective
NA = M .* (1.4/40);
f = Fieldin1x ./ M ./2;  %The Rayleigh distance in nm
w = sqrt(f .* lambda ./ pi);  % The radius of spot of laser in nm;
w = w ./1000; %w in um
depth = lambda * 1 ./ (NA.^2) ./ 1000; %Widefield depth in um;

plot(M,w,'r',M,depth,'b');
legend('SPIM''s laser thickness','Widefield''s depth')
xlabel('Magnificantion');
ylabel('Resolution/um')
